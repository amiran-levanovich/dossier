# DACH Conventions — Germany, Austria, Switzerland

European application norms differ from US/international ones, and the German-speaking market has its own expectations. This doc applies whenever the posting or company is DACH-based; the base rules in `cv_rules.md` / `cover_letter_rules.md` still hold underneath.

## Language

- German posting → German documents (CV as *Lebenslauf*, cover letter as *Anschreiben*), unless the posting is explicitly bilingual or the company language is stated as English. English posting from a DACH company → English documents are fine.
- Formal register: **Sie**, never du, unless the posting itself uses du (common in startups — then mirror it).
- Anschreiben salutation: a named contact (`Sehr geehrte Frau X`) beats `Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren` — check the posting and company page for a name during Step 4 research.

## Lebenslauf (CV) specifics

- **Tabular, reverse-chronological** is the expected shape. Two pages is normal for senior profiles (the one-page US convention does not bind here); over two is still too much.
- **Photo, birth date, nationality, marital status:** legally no longer required (AGG — the German anti-discrimination act) but a professional photo is still *common and often expected in practice*, especially at traditional companies. This is the **user's choice**, recorded once in `knowledge/profile.md` — ask during intake, don't re-ask per application. Never include marital status; birth date and nationality only if the user opted in.
- **Signature and date** at the bottom of the Lebenslauf are a traditional touch still appreciated by conservative employers — user's choice.
- **No employment gaps left silent.** German recruiters read for gaps; anything over ~2–3 months should be named honestly (sabbatical, job search, further training, care work). Gaps are in the KB from intake; handling is a per-application judgment surfaced to the user.
- **Zeugnisse culture:** German applications traditionally attach *Arbeitszeugnisse* (employer reference letters) and degree certificates. If the user has them, note their existence in `knowledge/profile.md`; when a portal asks for "vollständige Bewerbungsunterlagen", that means CV + Anschreiben + Zeugnisse.

## Protected titles — hard rule

Some titles are legally protected in the region and **must never be applied to a person who doesn't hold the qualification**, regardless of ATS keyword benefit:

- **"Ingenieur/in"** (and compounds) — protected in Germany and Austria; requires an accredited engineering degree. A software developer without one is a *Softwareentwickler/in* or *Developer* — never *Engineer* in German-language documents. (An English-language CV using the industry-standard "Software Engineer" for a developer role is a gray zone many use — but if the user's KB `constraints.md` says the distinction matters to them or the degree situation is checkable, respect the stricter reading.)
- **"Doktor/Dr."**, state-certified titles (*staatlich geprüft*), and similar credentials — only with the actual credential.

The intake interview records the user's degree situation and title rules in `knowledge/constraints.md`; the verifier treats a violation as a BLOCKER.

## Anschreiben specifics

- Still expected in most German applications — **no Anschreiben ≈ near-zero conversion** at traditional companies; only skip if the portal literally has no field for it.
- One page maximum; the 6-part formula from `cover_letter_rules.md` applies, in German.
- **The logistics close is not optional here:** work permit status (for non-EU citizens this is often the first filter), notice period (*Kündigungsfrist*) or earliest start date, and — if the posting asks, which is common — the salary expectation (*Gehaltsvorstellung*), given as a bracketed annual gross range anchored at the target from `knowledge/goals.md`.

## Austria & Switzerland deltas

- **Austria:** as Germany, plus postings legally state a minimum salary (*KV-Minimum*) — the real offer is negotiable above it; don't anchor to the stated minimum.
- **Switzerland:** photo still very common; state work-permit status prominently (non-EU permits are a major filter); salaries are stated monthly ×13 in some industries — clarify the basis before naming numbers. German-speaking Swiss companies accept High German documents.

## Salary conventions

Annual gross (*Brutto/Jahr*) in Germany and Austria; state a range, never a point ("62.000–68.000 € brutto/Jahr"), anchored at the top of the user's target from `goals.md`. Only include it when asked for; volunteering it in the Anschreiben unprompted is a mild negative signal.

## Contract clause taxonomy — for the offer stage

Used by `lifecycle/offer.md` during the clause walk: the clauses to *expect* in a DACH employment contract and the common patterns to compare against. **Boundary, absolute:** every row describes market patterns for orientation — none is a statement of what the law requires, allows, or would enforce. The comparison output is always a neutral tag and a question, never a verdict; anything touching legality or enforceability goes to the lawyer list *unanswered*.

| Clause (German name)                                        | What to look for                                                                                                                                                       |
| :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| *Befristung* (fixed term)                                   | Permanent (*unbefristet*) or fixed-term — and if fixed, whether a reason is named. A fixed term where a permanent role was discussed is a promise mismatch.            |
| *Probezeit* (probation)                                     | Commonly up to 6 months, with a shortened notice period during it. Note the length and the in-probation notice terms.                                                 |
| *Kündigungsfrist* (notice period)                           | Common pattern: ~4 weeks early on, contractually lengthened with tenure. Check whether extended periods bind **both** sides symmetrically — one-sided length is an `attention` tag. |
| *Vergütung* (salary & installments)                         | Annual gross split into 12, 13, or 14 installments. A 13th salary can be contractual, "voluntary" (*Freiwilligkeitsvorbehalt* — revocable wording), or absent; which one it is changes what the number in the offer letter actually means. |
| Bonus / variable pay                                        | Target amount, conditions, payout timing — and whether the wording is contractual or discretionary (*freiwillig*, *ohne Rechtsanspruch*). Discretionary wording around a number that was quoted as fixed is a classic promise mismatch. |
| *Arbeitszeit & Überstunden* (working time & overtime)       | "Overtime compensated by salary" (*mit dem Gehalt abgegolten*) clauses are common; note whether the covered amount is bounded or open-ended.                            |
| *Urlaub* (vacation)                                         | 25–30 days is the common full-time pattern seen in offers. How contractual days interact with statutory minimums is a lawyer question, not a tag.                       |
| Remote / *Homeoffice*                                       | Frequently promised verbally and **absent from the contract** — the single most common notable-absence finding. A policy reference ("per company policy, revocable") is weaker than a contractual clause; tag the difference. |
| *Nebentätigkeit* (side work)                                | Approval or notification clauses are common; note whether approval is required or merely notification.                                                                  |
| *Verschwiegenheit* / IP assignment                          | Confidentiality duration and scope; invention/IP clauses. Broad scope is an `attention` tag and often lawyer-list material.                                             |
| *Nachvertragliches Wettbewerbsverbot* (post-contract non-compete) | The common pattern pairs it with compensation (*Karenzentschädigung*). Scope, duration, and the compensation terms are **always** lawyer-list material — never assess enforceability. |
| *Vertragsstrafe* (contractual penalty)                      | Penalties for non-start or early exit. Amount and trigger noted; assessment goes to the lawyer list.                                                                     |
| *Ausschlussfristen* (claim cut-off periods)                 | Short contractual deadlines for asserting claims — easy to miss, worth a plain-language description whenever present.                                                    |

**Austria:** expect the collective-agreement classification (*KV-Einstufung*) stated in the contract, and 13th + 14th salary as the standard pattern under most KVs — an offer quoted "×14" is not comparable 1:1 to a German "×12" number. **Switzerland:** 13th salary common; clarify whether quoted figures are monthly ×12 or ×13 before comparing anything.
